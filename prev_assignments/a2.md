 1. Validation Dataset
Our goal is to evaluate how well language models can align K–12 math problems with the correct Common Core State Standards (CCSS). To do this, we needed a set of human-labeled problems to serve as ground truth, so we created one ourselves. The four of us (Krish, Sera, Zane, and our teacher buddy) each annotated 25 problems from the MathFish train split, a dataset of 13,065 math problems. 20 problems were shared across all four annotators for IRR purposes, and each person annotated 5 additional unique problems to broaden coverage. This gives us 20 distinct annotated problems in total. Problem text and metadata are stored in annotations/problems.json, the per-person assignment breakdown is in annotations/assignments.json, and each annotator’s labels are in annotations/<n>_annotations.jsonl.
Codebook
Our codebook is the CCSS hierarchy from Achieve the Core, stored in standards.jsonl (737 entries, 385 unique standards). Standards follow a four-level structure: grade, domain (example: NBT = Number and Operations in Base Ten), cluster (example: A), and the specific standard (example: 4.NBT.A.1). For each problem, we assigned one or more standard codes along with a relation type indicating how the problem connects to that standard:
Addressing: the primary standard the problem is assessing
Building On: a prerequisite standard the student needs to draw on
Building Towards: a standard the problem scaffolds toward but does not fully assess
For all benchmarking and IRR calculations, we treat Addressing labels as ground truth, since they most directly reflect what a problem is testing.
Annotator Training
Since memorizing 385 standards isn’t realistic, we built a browser based annotation tool (annotate.py + static/index.html) with hierarchical navigation: annotators select a grade level, then drill down through domain → cluster → standard. This keeps the number of visible options manageable at each step and mirrors how teachers naturally think about content standards. Before starting independent annotation, all four of us reviewed the codebook together and worked through several practice problems as a group to calibrate on edge cases, particularly problems that span multiple standards or require recognizing implicit prerequisite knowledge.
Problem Selection and Dataset Size
We sampled from the MathFish train split using the filtering criteria in setup_annotations.py (lines 19–41). We kept only problems that: (1) had existing publisher-provided standard labels (allowing us to sanity-check our annotations against an external reference), (2) were text-only with no images, (3) were non-duplicate, and (4) were between 20 and 2,000 characters long. The filtered pool was then stratified by grade level and shuffled with seed=42 to ensure our sample covered a range of grades.
We annotated 25 problems per person in line with the assignment guideline of 20–25 for analytic classification tasks. The 20 shared problems give all four annotators enough overlap to compute a reliable IRR signal, while the 5 unique problems per person extend breadth across additional grade bands and problem types. While 40 total problems is a modest validation set, it is consistent with comparable annotation studies and sufficient to meaningfully evaluate model performance at multiple levels of the CCSS hierarchy.
2. Inter-Rater Reliability
We measured IRR using Krippendorff’s alpha: suited for four raters, multi-label nominal annotations across 20 shared items. Unlike Cohen’s kappa (pairwise only), it generalizes to n raters and handles missing data. The formula is α = 1 − (Dₒ/Dₑ), where Dₒ is observed disagreement and Dₑ is chance-expected disagreement. Values above 0.8 indicate reliable agreement, 0.667–0.8 tentative, and below 0.667 marginal (Krippendorff, 2004).

Analysis
All three LLMs substantially outperform TF-IDF on standard-level F1 (0.455-0.500 vs. 0.119), which confirms that simple word overlap isn’t enough, matching problems to standards requires actual semantic understanding. That said, the models show pretty different precision recall tradeoffs. GPT-5.2 gets the best overall F1 at every granularity level (domain F1 = 0.643) by predicting more standards and casting a wider net. Gemini is the most precise model (standard precision = 0.682) but has the lowest recall (0.341 at the standard level); it tends to pick one confident standard rather than committing to a full set. Claude Sonnet 4.6 lands in between, and notably achieves the highest exact match at the cluster and domain levels (0.475 and 0.525), meaning it more consistently lands in the right neighborhood even when the exact standard codes differ.
F1 improves consistently from standard to domain level across all models, which mirrors the IRR pattern; fine-grained prediction is hard for humans and models alike. Exact match at the standard level stays low across the board (0.35-0.40), which tells us that fully recovering a problem’s complete standard set from text alone is still an unsolved problem.
Error Analysis
Two error patterns come up consistently across models. The first is multi-label under-prediction: when a problem touches multiple standards, models almost always anchor on the most obvious one and miss the rest. This shows up as a persistent gap between precision and recall for all three LLMs. The second is within-cluster substitution: models frequently predict a standard from the right cluster but the wrong specific code. Both patterns are consistent with the MathFish paper’s finding that GPT-4 predicted standards that were “conceptually close but subtly wrong”, our results with GPT-5.2, Gemini, and Claude confirm this holds across newer models too.
Looking at the per-problem predictions, cross-domain errors also follow a clear pattern. The most common mistake is OA→NBT (9 counts): problems labeled as Operations & Algebraic Thinking get predicted as Number & Operations in Base Ten, both involve whole-number computation, so the surface language overlaps heavily. NS→OA (5 counts) is the next most frequent. The EE domain has the most scattered errors, with mispredictions spread across N-RN, A-SSE, and F-IF, suggesting that algebraic reasoning problems are the hardest to categorize. Geometry and Fractions are the cleanest domains with the fewest off-diagonal errors.

4. Track Choice: Build a Model (Track 2)
We choose Track 2: Build a Model. While frontier LLMs show meaningful lift over lexical baselines, standard-level F1 of 0.455-0.500 and exact match rates of 35-40% leave substantial room for improvement. The error patterns, multi-label under-prediction, within-cluster substitution, and domain confusion (especially OA/NBT and EE)  are systematic and tractable.
Three Proposed Improvements
Hierarchical Prediction: Decompose prediction into domain → cluster → standard. Each stage conditions on the previous, reducing the label space and directly addressing OA/NBT and EE domain confusion by constraining predictions to a coherent subtree of the CCSS hierarchy.
Retrieval-Augmented Few-Shot Prompting: Retrieve the k most similar annotated problems by embedding similarity and include them as few-shot examples. This calibrates the model on how many standards to predict, mitigating multi-label under-prediction.
Standard Description Conditioning: Augment prompts with full CCSS descriptions from standards.jsonl. Richer semantic signals help distinguish near-neighbor standards that share surface keywords but differ in cognitive demand, directly addressing within-cluster substitutions.
Construct validity will be evaluated by measuring alignment between model predictions and MathFish publisher labels as a reference instrument, and by correlating model confidence with annotator agreement rates per standard (Kane, 2006). Anticipated limitations: (a) the 40-problem validation set yields high-variance estimates; (b) hierarchical prediction may propagate errors, a wrong domain forecloses the correct standard; (c) few-shot retrieval quality depends on annotation pool breadth, which we plan to expand.
