Figure 1 shows that linguistic complexity increases monotonically from early elementary to high school. Early grades (1-3) average a Flesch Reading Ease score of 72.8, while high school courses average 63.8. This 9-point decrease indicates that the problems high schoolers have contain harder text. Geometry problems are the most linguistically complex (59.3), likely because they use technical vocabulary like "perpendicular" and "congruent." Problem length also increases substantially: grade 1 problems average 28 words while Algebra 1 problems average 54 words. One thing to consider is that the more complex mathematical terms in higher grade levels might account for much of this increased complexity rather than the problem wording itself.
We learned that the curriculum maintains consistent linguistic scaffolding, as there are no sudden spikes in reading difficulty within grade bands. We also learned that reading level might be a hidden barrier in math education: students might understand the math, but be unsuccessful in solving the problem because it’s beyond their reading level. Figure 2 displays a heatmap of how frequently the top 30 Common Core standards appear together on the same problem. The strongest co-occurrences reveal natural prerequisite clusters: 5.NBT.A.2 (powers of 10) and 5.NBT.A.1 (place value) co-occur 50 times, while 3.OA.A.1 (multiplication) and 3.OA.A.2 (division) co-occur 42 times. These pairings reflect how curricula bundle conceptually related skills. Cross-grade co-occurrences like 3.OA.C.7 (multiply/divide fluency) appearing with 4.NBT.B.5 (multi-digit multiplication) 19 times suggest explicit "building on" relationships.
This analysis is mostly confirmation that Common Core standards are organized coherently: rather than uncovering any hidden structure, we’ve confirmed the curriculum's intentional design. Figure 3 uses TF-IDF to identify distinctive vocabulary across three problem types. Practice problems (n=4,386) emphasize product-oriented terms: "equation," "graph," "triangle," "area", which makes sense because they are used as skill drills. Lesson activities (n=5,978) use process and collaboration terms like "discussion," "partner," "share," "display," "access", which indicates teacher-facilitated exploration. With regard to teaching, we learned that the prevalence of social language in lesson activities ("partner," "share") reflects a constructivist pedagogy, i.e. these lessons are being done in groups, not just individually.

When looking at the language of the problems, the more successful examples were the ones that clearly said what information was given and what the student was supposed to find. For example, im_practice_004134 (the scale image of a book) clearly tells the student the size of the image and the real width of the book, and then asks two simple questions: what scale is being used and how tall the real book is. The wording is clear and specific, so it is easy to understand what the problem is asking.

A less successful example is im_practice_005415, which asks how many “different” 35° angles can be made using a ray. The problem never explains what “different” means, which makes it confusing. A student might not know if angles that are rotated or flipped count as different, or what kind of answer the problem wants. This makes the student spend time trying to understand the question instead of focusing on the math.

These differences can be explained by how much extra thinking the language of the problem causes. When a problem clearly explains the goal and the rules, students can focus on solving it. When the language is unclear, students have to guess what is expected or wait for the teacher to explain. Because of this, problems with clear and direct language are easier for students to work on by themselves, while problems with unclear wording usually need more teacher help.

Hypotheses. Building on your thoughts from A0, elaborate on the construct you plan to analyze and/or support going forward. How is this construct discussed in education literature? Cite three sources.


We hypothesize that LLMs can actually predict the knowledge prerequisites required to solve a math problem by first analyzing the problem text - this is important because although Mathfish aligns problems to standards, it doesn't actually tell us what students need to already know before they can engage with the problem. Although there might be standards online for how problems are correlated with knowledge prerequisites, teachers don't have the time to trace through every single problem they assign and so we think this is a perfect application for using LLMs.


Our hypothesis is grounded in established research - Kenneth Kodinger, for example, found in his Knowledge-Learning-Instruction Framework paper that it’s possible to break math problem-solving into knowledge components or essentially discrete cognitive units that students need to complete a task. And his research showed that student errors usually stem from missing prerequisites rather than the target skill - as an example, problem errors occur frequently because students don't know that “of” means you should multiply (rather than them not being able to actually multiply two numbers). Karen Hess and Douglas Clements found similar conclusions in their own literature and so in our project, we aim to test whether LLMs can actually capture these prerequisite relationships and compare that against what experts find.
